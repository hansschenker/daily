A Complete Blood Count (CBC) test is a common blood test that gives doctors an overview of a patient's general health status. It measures several components of the blood, including:

Red blood cells (RBCs): These cells carry oxygen from the lungs to the rest of the body. A CBC measures not just the number of RBCs, but also certain characteristics of these cells, such as their size and volume.

Hemoglobin (Hb): The protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen. Low hemoglobin usually suggests anemia.

Hematocrit (Hct): The proportion of your total blood volume that's made up by red blood cells. Low hematocrit also suggests anemia.

Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV): A measurement of the average size of your RBCs. Abnormalities can indicate certain types of anemia.

Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin (MCH) and Mean Corpuscular Hemoglobin Concentration (MCHC): These measurements relate to the amount of hemoglobin in the RBCs. Abnormalities can also indicate certain types of anemia.

White blood cells (WBCs): These cells fight infection. The test measures the total number of these cells and sometimes breaks them down into types:

A change in the number of white blood cells could indicate an infection, inflammation, autoimmune disease, or other medical conditions.

Platelets: These help the blood to clot. A CBC gives the number of platelets in your blood. Low platelet count can risk bleeding too much during surgery, and high count could increase the risk of blood clots.

Mean Platelet Volume (MPV): This measures the average size of your platelets. New platelets are larger, and an increased MPV can mean that your body is producing them faster than usual, which could be a response to an inflammation or a blood loss.

A Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP) is a blood test that provides a snapshot of your metabolic status, including kidney function, blood sugar levels, and electrolyte balance. It consists of the following components or biomarkers:

Glucose: Glucose is the primary source of energy for the body's cells. High levels may indicate diabetes or prediabetes, while low levels can indicate hypoglycemia.

Calcium: Calcium is an essential mineral in the body, playing a critical role in muscle contraction, nerve function, and blood clotting. Abnormal levels can indicate a range of conditions affecting the bones, kidneys, pancreas, or parathyroid glands.

Electrolytes:

Sodium: Sodium is a mineral that plays a vital role in fluid balance and nerve and muscle function. Abnormal levels can be seen in various conditions such as dehydration, kidney disease, or disorders of the adrenal glands.

Potassium: Potassium is also crucial for nerve and muscle cell functioning, particularly for the heart. Abnormal levels can affect the heart rhythm and are often associated with kidney disease.

Bicarbonate (or Total CO2): Bicarbonate is an electrolyte that helps maintain the pH and acid-base balance in your body. Changes in its levels can be related to lung or kidney disease, certain toxin exposures, or an imbalance in other electrolytes.

Chloride: Chloride, like sodium, helps to regulate the body's fluid balance and usually changes proportionally to sodium.

Kidney Tests:

Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN): Urea nitrogen is a waste product from the breakdown of protein in the body. Normally, the kidneys remove urea nitrogen from the blood, but when kidney function is reduced, BUN levels rise.

Creatinine: Creatinine is a waste product produced in the muscles and removed from the blood by the kidneys. High levels can indicate a problem with kidney function.

The BMP may also include the Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR), which is calculated from the creatinine test result and provides an estimate of how well the kidneys are filtering waste from the blood.

It's worth mentioning that results from a BMP should be interpreted in the context of a patient's overall health, medical history, and other test results. Abnormal results can signify a problem that needs to be addressed and may require further investigation.

A lipoprotein panel, also known as a lipid panel, is a blood test that measures several different types of cholesterol and fats (lipids) in the blood. Here are the main biomarkers included in a standard lipoprotein panel:

Total Cholesterol: This measures all the cholesterol in all the lipoprotein particles together. High levels of total cholesterol can be indicative of an increased risk of heart disease.

Low-Density Lipoprotein Cholesterol (LDL-C): Often referred to as "bad cholesterol," LDL carries cholesterol to your arteries, where it can collect in the artery walls and contribute to plaque formation, a condition known as atherosclerosis. Higher levels of LDL-C are associated with an increased risk of heart disease.

High-Density Lipoprotein Cholesterol (HDL-C): Known as "good cholesterol," HDL carries cholesterol away from the arteries and back to the liver, where it's broken down and removed from the body. High levels of HDL-C are associated with a lower risk of heart disease.

Triglycerides: Triglycerides are a type of fat found in your blood that your body uses for energy. Elevated levels can lead to an increased risk of heart disease, especially when accompanied by high LDL-C and low HDL-C levels.

Very-Low-Density Lipoprotein (VLDL-C): This is often estimated as a percentage of your triglyceride value. VLDL-C carries triglycerides to tissues and eventually becomes LDL. High levels are associated with an increased risk of heart disease.

Non-HDL Cholesterol: This is your total cholesterol minus your HDL-C. The result indicates your level of "bad" cholesterol. The higher the number, the higher the risk of heart disease.

These values can provide a comprehensive view of a person's lipid levels, and they can help doctors determine a person's risk of developing heart disease and other cardiovascular conditions. It's important to note that these values should not be viewed in isolation but should be considered as part of an overall health assessment. Lifestyle, genetics, and other health conditions also play a significant role in cardiovascular risk.

A coagulation panel, also known as coagulation tests or clotting tests, are a series of blood tests that evaluate how well your blood clotting process is functioning. These tests can help diagnose blood clotting disorders and assess your risk of excessive bleeding or developing clots.

Here are the main biomarkers evaluated in a coagulation panel:

Prothrombin Time (PT): This test measures the time it takes for your blood to clot. It primarily assesses the function of clotting factors VII, V, and X, prothrombin, and fibrinogen. It's often used to monitor patients taking warfarin, a blood-thinning medication.

International Normalized Ratio (INR): This is a calculation based on the PT test and provides a standardized method of reporting the effects of an oral anticoagulant such as warfarin on blood clotting. High INR values may indicate a high risk of bleeding, while low values may point to a higher risk of clotting.

Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (aPTT): This test measures the time it takes for your blood to clot and primarily evaluates clotting factors XII, XI, IX, VIII, X, V, prothrombin, and fibrinogen. It's often used to monitor patients on heparin, another blood-thinning medication.

Thrombin Time (TT): This test measures the time it takes for a clot to form in the plasma of a blood sample after thrombin has been added. It is used to evaluate fibrinogen, a protein in the body that helps blood to clot.

Fibrinogen Test: Fibrinogen is a protein made by the liver. Fibrinogen helps stop bleeding by helping blood clots to form. An abnormal fibrinogen level can affect blood clotting.

D-dimer Test: This test measures the amount of D-dimer, a substance in the blood that's produced when a blood clot dissolves in the body. It's often used to help rule out the presence of an inappropriate blood clot (thrombus).

Factor assays: These are specialized tests to evaluate the function or quantity of specific clotting factors if a coagulation disorder is suspected.

Please note that the relevance and need for these tests depend on your specific situation, symptoms, and medical history. Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) and Vitamin D - these help regulate calcium and phosphate balance in the body. Imbalances can affect bone health and cause symptoms like muscle cramps or weakness.

Melatonin - a hormone that regulates the sleep-wake cycle. It might be tested in people with suspected sleep disorders.

Gastrin - a hormone that stimulates secretion of gastric acid. It may be tested if a person is suspected of having an overproduction of stomach acid or a certain type of stomach tumor called a gastrinoma.

These are just a few examples - many other hormones can also be tested depending on the specific circumstances. It's important to discuss with a healthcare provider which tests are most appropriate based on symptoms, medical history, and overall health.
